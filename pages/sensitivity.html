R.6: Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis

Hyperparameters

The robust MoE objective introduces a single new hyperparameter beyond those of standard MoE training:

\(\Gamma\): The density ratio bound that controls the size of the DRO uncertainty set \(W_\Gamma\). The adversary can place weight up to \(\Gamma\) on any example, so at most a \(1/\Gamma\) fraction of the data receives elevated weight. During training, we average the top-\(\lceil n/\Gamma \rceil\) losses in each minibatch. We select \(\Gamma\) by minimizing validation ECE on a held-out 10% split of the CIFAR-10H training data.

We also use one training schedule parameter that is standard in distributionally robust optimization:

\(n_{\text{warmup}}\): Number of ERM pre-training epochs before switching to the robust objective. ERM warmup is standard practice in DRO to allow the model to learn useful representations before the robust reweighting takes effect. Warmup is selected jointly with \(\Gamma\) by the same validation criterion. Higher \(\Gamma\) seems to require more warmup, perhaps since more aggressive reweighting requires more stable representations to perform well.

All other hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, number of experts, router architecture, backbone, weight decay, optimizer, scheduler) are shared with vanilla MoE and are held constant across all methods in our experiments.

Validation-based hyperparameter selection

We select \((\Gamma, n_{\text{warmup}})\) jointly by minimizing ECE on a held-out 10% split of the CIFAR-10H training data. This validation set is drawn from the training distribution and contains no subpopulation shift information.

We run validation over a grid of 8 \(\Gamma\) values × 8 \(n_{\text{warmup}}\) values, each trained with 5 seeds. The top 5 configurations by validation ECE are:

Sensitivity Analysis

Sensitivity of \(\Gamma\) and \(n_{warmup}\) parameters

We sweep \(\Gamma \in \{1.0, 1.1, 1.2, 1.4, 1.5, 1.6, 1.9, 2.0\}\) and warmup \(\in \{0, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 40\}\) on CIFAR-10H, training 5 seeds per configuration (320 runs total).

The left heatmap shows that Robust MoE achieves good Hard ECE across a broad region of the grid, across \(\Gamma \in [1.2, 1.6]\) and warmup \(\in [0, 30]\). Comparing this to the right heatmap reveals the core tradeoff, where configurations with the best Hard ECE (larger \(\Gamma\), moderate warmup) have degraded overall ECE.

In these plots, we hold one parameter constant at its default value and sweep the other parameter to understand its effect. Holding warmup=30, Hard ECE improves from \(\Gamma = 1.0\) to roughly \(\Gamma = 1.8\), then degrades at higher values. Overall ECE degrades sharply above \(\Gamma = 1.4\). Holding \(\Gamma = 1.2\), Hard ECE is best at warmup \(\in [0, 15]\) with smooth degradation outside this range, and Overall ECE is best at warmup \(\in [20, 30]\). Accuracy remains stable across warmup \(\in [20, 40]\).
